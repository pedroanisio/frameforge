## I · The Letter — Johnston, 1906

**The skeleton vs. the tool (§1.2).** Johnston defines the essential forms as
"the necessary parts" — the structural plan of an alphabet. The essential form
is "a purely abstract form, the common property of every rational age and
country," while its characterisation — weight, contrast, serifs, voice — "is
mainly the product of tools and materials." *Reading:* design is what happens
between the skeleton and the surface.

- "One of the finest things the letter-craftsman can do, is to make the Essential
  Forms of letters beautiful in themselves." (on simplicity, ch. XIV)

**Lineage (§1.1).** "the Roman Capitals have held the supreme place among letters
for readableness and beauty." Our lowercase is formalised handwriting; our
capitals are architecture. "We have an excellent precedent in the Italian scribes
who went back 300 years for a model and gave us the Roman small-letter as a
result." (Preface)

**The tool makes the letter (§1.3).** A broad pen held at a constant angle gives
"a thin horizontal stroke and a thick vertical stroke" without any decision by
the writer. Slant the pen and the axis of weight rotates with it. Most historical
differences between one class of type and the next are a change in one variable:
the angle, breadth and freedom of the tool.

**The serif (§1.4).** Written with a formal pen the skeleton "looks incomplete
and unfinished; and it is obvious that the thin strokes, at least, require marked
terminals or serifs." "serifs are treated as the actual finishing and shaping of
the ends of the stems and branches, rather than as added parts." Corollary:
"serifs of some sort are practically essential to the proper characterisation of
an alphabet" — even the sans serif is a decision about serifs.

**The nine qualities (§1.6, ch. XIV)** — the ranked table; *the order is the
argument.* "The first general virtue of lettering is readableness, the second
fitness for a given Use." Readableness is "the only sound basis for a practical
theory of lettering"; character is "the product of a particular hand & tool at
work in a particular craft" and comes last — style is earned, not applied. Usable
"as a test of the qualities of any piece of lettering — whether Manuscript,
Printing, or Engraving."

| Virtue | # · Quality | Definition (Johnston) |
|---|---|---|
| **Readableness** | 1 · Simplicity | having no unnecessary parts |
| | 2 · Distinctiveness | the distinguishing marks of each letter strongly made |
| | 3 · Proportion | no part of a letter wrongly exaggerated or dwarfed |
| **Beauty** | 4 · Beauty of Form | each letter an individual and living whole, not a collection of parts |
| | 5 · Beauty of Uniformity | the family likeness of letters, so that they go well together |
| | 6 · Beauty of Arrangement | fitness in placing, connecting and spacing; in the disposal of the whole |
| **Character** | 7 · Essential Qualities of the Hand | genuine work, the direct outcome of a rightly handled tool |
| | 8 · Freedom | skilled and unaffected boldness |
| | 9 · Personality | the marks that distinguish one hand from another |

- Uniformity (§1.6/§ beauty): "Where the text letters are uniform, the reader is
  free to give his attention to the sense of the words"; "the variations in an
  irregular or changing text are distracting."
- Character, honoured but placed last: "The whole duty of beautiful typography is
  not to substitute for the beauty or interest of the thing thought … a beauty or
  interest of its own, but … to win access for that communication by the clearness
  and beauty of the vehicle." (T. J. Cobden-Sanderson, _The Book Beautiful_, 1902,
  as quoted by Johnston.)

**Anatomy (§1.5, §1.7).** Five governing lines — ascender, cap-height, x-height,
baseline, descender; the ratio of x-height to cap-height is the strongest
determinant of how big a face looks. Compare faces on x-height, never on point
size. Capital widths after the monumental model: round letters (O Q C G D) fill a
square; H A N T U V ≈ four-fifths; B E F L P R S ≈ one-half; M beyond the square,
W well beyond; I and J are strokes. "The proportions of large CAPITALS … follow
the fine monumental type." Even spacing cannot rescue uneven proportion.

**Taxonomy (§1.8).** Classes of type are episodes in the biography of the tool —
garalde/old-style, didone/modern, grotesque & neo-grotesque, humanist sans,
geometric sans, monospace. Modern class names after Bringhurst.

**Weight (§1.9).** A modern typeface is a family spanning an axis of weight. Two
rules: hierarchy moves one step at a time (regular vs. semibold = structure;
regular vs. black = noise); and **never synthesise** — "a slanted roman is not an
italic and a smeared regular is not a bold," both forgeries the eye convicts.

**Scale (§1.10).** A modular scale — base ≈ 11.5, ratio a chosen voice: 1.2 quiet,
1.25 assertive, 1.333 editorial, 1.5 poster. Every size is the ratio's power;
scale is uniformity (quality 5) applied to size.

**Spacing (§1.11, ch. IV).** "The letters of a word are fitted together so that
there is a general effect of evenness." Capitals want air; small letters want
none; the word-space is a letter too. Distinctiveness "necessitates sufficient
interspaces" — sufficiency, not surplus. Test: squint until the block is one grey
— dark knots are crowding, white rivers excess.

**Measure & leading (§1.12).** Body measure 45–75 characters. Measure and leading
are one system: widen the line and you must open the leading; a wide page wants
columns or larger type, never merely longer lines.

**Margins (§1.13, ch. VI).** "Margins are necessary in order to isolate and frame
a text: thus they contribute to its legibility and beauty." Canon for the opening:
inner 1½ + 1½, top 2, outer 3, foot 4 — "proportions … common in early MSS." The
opening (the two-page spread), not the page, is the unit; foot widest, gutter
halves, top tightens.

**Chapter I in five lines.** (i) The skeleton is common property; the tool gives
it voice. (ii) Readableness first, fitness second — beauty and character serve
them. (iii) Serifs, weight and contrast are the fossil record of the pen.
(iv) Uniformity frees the reader; proportion is the alphabet's justice.
(v) Space — in the word, the line, the margin — is a material, not an absence.

## II · The Hue — Chevreul, 1839

**Vocabulary (§2.1, §§122–127).** "The words tones and hues recur continually,
both in common language and in that of artists; yet they are not so well defined
as to be free from ambiguity." Three terms:

- **Tone** — the modifications a colour "in its greatest intensity, is capable of
  receiving from white, which lowers its tone, or of black, which heightens it."
- **Scale** — "the assemblage of tones of the same colour, thus modified. The pure
  colour is the normal tone of the scale."
- **Hue** — "the modifications which a colour receives from the addition of a
  small quantity of another."

*Reading:* three separate axes the eye blurs — how light/dark (tone), which family
(scale), which way it leans (hue). The normal tone sits at different ranks per
scale (yellow peaks while light, blue arrives deep); scales are not
interchangeable ladders.

**The wheel (§2.2).** The painter's wheel — red, yellow, blue as primaries;
orange, green, violet mixed between; twelve named stations. Neighbours share
pigment; opposites share nothing. Not physics (light's spectrum is a line, not a
loop) but a fact of pigment and perception. Distance on the wheel = difference to
the eye.

**The law (§2.3, Part I, ch. II).** "All the phenomena I have observed seem to
depend upon a very simple law: when two contiguous colours are seen at the same
time, they appear as dissimilar as possible, both with regard to their optical
composition and their depth of tone." (M. E. Chevreul, 1839.) Two blades:
*contrast of tone* (beside a dark, a colour looks lighter; beside a light,
darker) and *contrast of colour* (each neighbour tints the other with its own
complement). No colour on any page is ever seen alone; the ground is always a
co-author. The discovery began as a complaint: Gobelins weavers found black yarns
feeble beside blue — the dye was innocent, the neighbourhood guilty.

**Successive contrast (§2.4).** The fatigued eye discharges a colour's complement
as an afterimage. Practical rule: let a neutral interval discharge the eye before
the next strong statement (slide sequences, onboarding, packaging walls).

**Complementaries (§2.5).** Colour's strongest chord and its most dangerous: at
full strength and equal area they vibrate. Remedy Chevreul endorses — unbalance
them: one rules, the other visits, subdued in tone or confined to a line.

**The six harmonies (§2.6)** — two families, each species shown as chips in the
book. Analogy soothes (fails as dullness/sleep); contrast wakes (fails as noise).
Quotations are Chevreul's definitions of each harmony:

| # | Family | Harmony | Chevreul's definition (quoted) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Analogy | of scale | "the simultaneous view of different tones of the same scale, more or less approximating" (specify "equal and sufficiently numerous intervals") |
| 2 | Analogy | of hues | "the simultaneous view of tones of the same, or nearly the same depth, belonging to neighbouring scales" |
| 3 | Analogy | of a dominant coloured light | "various colours assorted according to the law of contrast, but one of them predominating, as would result from the view of these colours through a slightly-coloured glass" |
| 4 | Contrast | of scale | "the simultaneous view of two very distant tones of the same scale" |
| 5 | Contrast | of hues | "the simultaneous view of tones of different depths, belonging to neighbouring scales" (allied scales "very difficult to obtain" at equal strength; escape: "sacrifice one of the colours, which he subdues, to make the other more brilliant") |
| 6 | Contrast | of colours | "the simultaneous view of colours belonging to very distant scales, assorted according to the law of contrast" (complementary pairs are "an undoubtedly agreeable sensation") |

Every workable palette is a treaty between the two families.

**Neutrals (§2.7).** White lends every colour depth (darkens it by contrast of
tone); black lends brilliance and eats the deep tones; grey flatters everything
and competes with nothing — which is why proofing rooms and galleries are grey.

**Value does the work (§2.8) — the grey test.** Tone is the axis the eye trusts
first: in dim light, at distance, in poor print, and for readers whose hue is
unreliable. Design in tones first, then admit hue. Drain a layout of hue: if it
keeps its hierarchy it was built in tone; if it dissolves into one mass it trusted
hue alone.

**Colour meets the letter (§2.9).** Legibility is tone contrast between figure and
ground; modern practice asks **≥ 4.5:1** for body text (WCAG). Hue contrast alone
adds vibration but no legibility — red on green can vibrate at 1.4:1 and be
unreadable. Choose text and ground as tones first, then admit hue.

**Red, the working accent (§2.10).** The manuscript tradition is a complete theory
of accent colour: one hue reserved for structure (headings, initials, emphasis),
used sparingly enough that its every appearance is information. Johnston: "a page
of red (or blue) writing is very pleasant and effective, [and] we may certainly
take advantage of such a reasonable excuse for introducing it"; "Italics — either
in black or red — go best with Roman characters."

**The closed palette (§2.11).** The chapter compresses to one buildable object: a
ground (one paper, warm or cool — never two), an ink (one near-black; pure #000 is
harsher than any ink ever was), one accent (one hue, one duty), and quiet steps
(greys from the ink's own scale). Illustrative proportion: ground 62 %, text &
structure 30 %, accent 8 %. Expansion rule: a second accent = the first's
complement subdued, or a distant tone of the ink. "Closed" is the operative word —
a colour outside the set should mean something, exactly as an italic does.

**Never hue alone (§2.12).** Hue has no natural order and roughly one man in twelve
(≈ 8 %, National Eye Institute) has red and green as neighbours. Encode rank with
tone + position + label; then let hue sing over a structure that stands without
it. Chevreul's own division of labour: hue for character, tone for structure.

## III · The Arrangement — Batchelder (after Ross), 1904

**Pure design (§3.1).** "Pure design is the composition of tones, measures, shapes,
for the sake of rhythm, balance, harmony, the principles of order and beauty."
Three terms × three principles = a grid of nine; the scheme sounds austere and in
use is the opposite — it gives the designer what Johnston's table gave the letterer
and Chevreul's law gave the colourist: a small set of names for what the eye
already knows.

**The system (§3.2).** "a spot of paint may be described in three words; it is a
tone, a measure, a shape." Terms: tones (light & dark), measures (large & small),
shapes (this form and that). Principles: rhythm (regular change), balance (opposed
attractions), harmony (likeness in accord).

**Balance of tone (§3.3).** "Balance means repose that results from the opposition
of attractions." Everything pulls at the eye — the darker, the larger, the more
eccentric, the harder. Balanced when attractions oppose so completely that the eye
comes to rest; unbalanced when a pull goes unanswered. Tone balance = "the result
of a selection and arrangement of contrasts in such way that each part of a design
may keep its proper place without being unduly emphasized at the expense of other
parts." Black is the heaviest material on a white page; a little outweighs much
grey. Massed tones read at a glance; scattered, every fragment is an unanswered
attraction. A page's blacks are its display sizes and bolds; its greys the text;
its whites the margins — bold everything and you have told the reader nothing about
where to begin.

**Balance of measure — the steelyard (§3.4).** "The spots are to the eye precisely
like physical forces in nature, at least in so far as this law of balance applies."
Unequal spots balance like a man and a boy on a see-saw — the larger nearer the
fulcrum, the smaller farther, their moments (attraction × distance) agreeing — so
that "the point of balance of the measures shall coincide with the center of the
frame." It is a procedure, not a metaphor: weigh two areas, compute the balance
point as a physicist would, set the pair so it lands on the frame's centre.
Asymmetric layout is this steelyard worked at speed — a large image near the axis,
a small caption far from it, the page at rest without a single centred element.
Centring is the special case of equal arms; the general instrument is the beam.

**Balance of shape — symmetry (§3.5).** "Shape balance in design may be defined as
symmetry … shapes on one side are opposed by corresponding shapes on the other
side." "no difficulty will be experienced in finding examples of shape balance in
the work of all nations and periods." Measure balance offers "the same feeling of
repose that is found in symmetry" without the stiffness. Symmetry and asymmetry are
two solutions of one equation — mirrored vs. weighed — not rival styles.

**Rhythm (§3.6).** "Rhythm means joint action or movement, a consistent relation
and connection of parts that enables the eye to find a way through" the whole. Not
decoration but navigation. The pond image: "drop a stone into the water and as the
waves grow larger and larger you have measure rhythm, a movement by means of
interrelated measures." Two failures bound it — monotony (repetition without
change) and noise (change without repetition) — and "rhythm without balance is a
step toward 'mob rule.'" The page's own drum is its baselines at one leading.

**Harmony (§3.7).** "Harmony means the consistency of likeness; having something in
common … a unity, all the terms of which are in interior accord." Its practical
instrument is the common unit — "a common unit of measure, the quarter-inch of the
squared paper" — the ancestor, four decades early, of the baseline grid and the
8-point spacing system. Harmony is manufactured, not hoped for.

**One system, three crafts (§3.8).** Batchelder closes the loop himself: proportion
means the same thing "whether in a house, on the page of a book, or in the
formation of a" letter. The mapping (book's own table, Plate 48):

| Ross term | = | in typography & colour | traces to |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tones | weight & colour | type weight, ink greys, the accent | ch. I §1.9 · ch. II §2.8 |
| Measures | size & space | the modular scale, leading, margins | ch. I §1.10–1.13 |
| Shapes | form | letterforms, text blocks, plates | ch. I §1.2–1.5 |
| Rhythm | regular change | baselines, word-spaces, the scale | §3.6 |
| Balance | opposed attractions | the spread at rest; the steelyard | §3.3–3.5 |
| Harmony | likeness in accord | one family, one palette, one module | ch. I §1.6 · ch. II §2.11 |

**The grid (§3.9).** The modern grid is the steelyard institutionalised: a frame
that quantises attractions to a shared module before they argue, so the arithmetic
of balance is repeatable page after page, by many hands. Batchelder's quarter-inch
squared paper, Ross's common unit, the printer's em, the interface designer's
8-point step — one instrument, four names, a century of service.

## IV · Colour at Work — Chevreul applied

**The Gobelins complaint (§4.1).** The most consequential quality complaint in the
history of design was filed against a dye vat that was innocent: the defect was
optical, and the law of simultaneous contrast followed. Read this way, Chevreul is
the first design reviewer — handed a defect report and locating the bug in
perception rather than production. His own inventory: "I have thus been led to
extend it to the arts of tapestry" — and onward through carpets, dress, gardens,
apartments, picture-framing, calico, and printing with types.

**The room (§4.2).** Treat the interior as figure and ground at scale. The dominant
surface (the hangings) sets the scale; the border answers by contrast: "harmony of
contrast is the most suitable to papers of a uniform pure colour … consequently we
recommend for the dominant colour of the border the complementary of that of the
hangings." Warning against busy pattern: "patterns for hangings must recommend
themselves more by opposite colours than by minute finish in the details." Detail
invisible at wall distance is not richness, it is mud. *Reading:* one ground, one
answering accent, the rest from the ground's own scale — swap 'wall' for 'screen'
and the clause needs no edit.

**The garden (§4.3).** With the ground fixed by botany, "the grouping of flowers
will present no difficulty, since it will only be a simple conclusion from facts."
Complementary masses: "blue flowers and orange flowers, yellow flowers and violet
flowers." Red needs no partner planted — "rose or red flowers contrast with their
own leaves." Separations: "we must separate pink flowers from those that are either
scarlet or crimson; orange flowers from orange-yellow flowers; blue flowers from
violet-blue flowers." Green foliage is not noise; it is the ground that lets red
bloom unpartnered.

**The person (§4.4).** Whatever colour is worn near the face lends the face its
complement. "A delicate green is … favourable to all fair complexions which are
deficient in rose"; "Yellow imparts violet to a fair skin"; violet returns
"greenish-yellow"; "blue imparts orange, which is susceptible of allying itself
favourably … to the light flesh tints." Remedy for a failed pairing: an interposed
neutral — hair, ribbon, "wreaths or detached flowers" — i.e. a border, a keyline,
breathing space. Substitute 'avatar' for 'complexion': every profile picture sits
on a ground quietly recolouring it.

**The printed page (§4.5).** At the itinerary's end: black type on coloured paper.
The calico warning restated in every brand guideline since: "the pattern may appear
to be very different in colour from what was intended, and from what it really is."
Chevreul ranks nine papers by eye, "commencing with those which appeared to me the
easiest to read."

**1839 re-measured (§4.6).** His §516 legibility ranking (near-black ink on nine
coloured papers), re-measured against the WCAG relative-luminance formula, agrees
in **7 of 9 placings**; the two that move are the deep warm papers he flagged
himself, where hue pleads and luminance disagrees. The caveat every studio should
frame (§517): "I have every reason to believe that other eyes than mine would
require some change in the order I have assigned to the preceding assortments." And
the duration warning (§503): the strongest pairing "will be more favourable in a
reading of short duration, while it will be less so in a prolonged reading, because
… it will fatigue the eye more" — the whole paper-white-vs-dark-mode argument, run
once, by lamplight, in 1839.

**The method (§4.7).** Strip the century away and what remains is the working loop
of every design practice worth the name — and it is the same verification loop this
repo calls PALS's Law:

1. **The complaint** — someone trusts their eyes and says so.
2. **The audit** — test the material before the theory (the dye was acquitted).
3. **The law** — name the mechanism, not the symptom.
4. **The application** — walk the law through every trade it touches.
5. **The caveat** — publish the data *and* its observer ("other eyes than mine…").

"In viewing the law of simultaneous contrast of colours with reference to its
application, and in submitting to experiment all the consequences which appeared to
me to result from it, I have thus been led to extend it to the arts of tapestry."
(Chevreul, _Principles of Harmony and Contrast_ — Introduction.) A law that stops
at the laboratory door is a curiosity; Chevreul's walked out and has not been
unemployed since.
